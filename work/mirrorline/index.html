Mirrorline — auditing what AI assistants say about an organization

When someone asks an AI assistant about an organization, the answer is a reputation surface almost nobody is monitoring. Mirrorline probes that surface and audits it against the public record.

The
problem

A growing share of first impressions now happens inside AI assistants rather than on a search results page. Those answers are generated fresh, vary by model and phrasing, and can drift from what the documented record actually supports — an outdated fact, a confused entity, a claim with no visible source. Organizations monitor their press and their search results; the assistant layer mostly goes unwatched.

The
method

Structured probes — the questions real people plausibly ask about an organization — are put to AI assistants, and the answers are captured and decomposed into individual claims. Each claim is then audited against the documented public record. The divergence is the finding: what the record says, what the assistants tell people, and where the two split.

Provenance
discipline

Every claim in an answer gets a provenance classification: supported by an identifiable source, unsourced but consistent with the record, or unverifiable. The absence of a source is itself evidence — an assistant asserting something the record can't support is exactly the signal worth surfacing. The output is a reviewed brief built for a decision-maker, not a dashboard of scores.

Epistemic
honesty

A design principle carried over from my harmful-information work: the system produces signals for review, not determinations of truth. It does not promise control over what models say — nobody can honestly promise that — and it does not treat a divergence as a verdict. It flags, sources, and contextualises; judgment stays human. Designed to run in French and English.
