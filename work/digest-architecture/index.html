An executive-readout architecture for monitoring digests

A config-driven, multi-client monitoring engine that leads with judgment: the readout comes first, the evidence follows, and a language model never decides what matters.

The design
problem

Most automated digests are newsletters: a wall of story cards, ranked by recency or engagement, with the actual judgment — does anything here need attention? — left to the reader. That's backwards. A reader with ninety seconds needs the conclusion first: what crossed the bar today, why it matters, and what to consider doing about it. Everything else is supporting evidence.

The hard case is the quiet day. A system that pads an empty day with filler teaches its readers to skim; a system that can honestly say nothing crossed the bar today earns the right to be read carefully when something does. That case is part of the definition of done, with its own test.

The output
structure

Every edition renders in a fixed order: a one-line readout → priority findings, each as what happened / why it matters / a suggested step → a standing watch of items worth awareness but not action → an optional competitor watch → a coverage note stating what was scanned and what was dark, so the reader always knows the evidence base.

Determinism
first

The load-bearing decision: tier assignment is computed in code, before any model call. Keyword and weight scoring against per-client thresholds decides what surfaces and whether it lands in priority or standing watch. The model writes the analysis for each tier — it never assigns the tier. This is the same design rule as my harmful-information pipelines, applied to a new domain: the model summarises what passes the gate; it never decides what passes.

Multi-client
by config

One engine, many deployments. Each client is a YAML file: sources, priority terms and weights, thresholds, cadence — and a lens field that switches the analytical frame between narrative-risk and brand-reputation framing. The expertise lives in the config: the terms, weights, and prompt content per lens are the product; the engine just runs them. Per-client API keys isolate one deployment's credit exhaustion from the rest, and cheap deterministic pre-filters run before anything reaches an API.

Reliability
rules

Carried over from operating monitoring pipelines through a live emergency: send at most once — enforced by committed state files that survive scheduler retries and same-day races. Fail closed — an empty feed or malformed model output blocks the send rather than shipping a confident edition on a narrowed evidence base. A skipped edition is recoverable; a wrong one in an inbox is not.
