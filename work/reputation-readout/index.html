Research & Insight · Decision Support

From fragmented signals to an executive reputation readout

How I designed a recurring insights product that turns media, social, web and communications evidence into a clear narrative for senior leadership, not another dashboard.

The research
question

How can fragmented media, social, web and communications-performance data be synthesised into a concise, recurring assessment that helps senior leaders understand what is changing, why it matters, and where attention may be needed?

The work was not reporting. It meant deciding which signals were meaningful, how different sources should be read together, when a change was a real shift rather than normal fluctuation, how to separate reach from engagement from reputation from operational relevance, and how to communicate the limits of what the evidence could show, then turning that into recommendations rather than metric summaries.

The leadership problem Context, not confidential material

Too much
data, too
little meaning

At the IFRC, I produce a recurring executive insights product for senior leadership. The organisation operates across a global network of 191 National Societies and a communications environment that spans humanitarian emergencies, public policy, fundraising, leadership visibility and institutional reputation.

Communications teams had no shortage of channel and monitoring data. What leadership needed was the opposite of more numbers: a repeatable structure that could bring several evidence streams into one view, and stay concise enough to support a senior-level conversation.

No single metric is treated as a measure of impact. High engagement is not automatically strategic value; low public engagement does not mean a message failed to reach a priority audience; media volume does not necessarily translate into trust, action or fundraising. An apparent shift needs context before it becomes a finding.

Method Decision → evidence → synthesis → judgment → narrative
1
Start with the decision

Define what leadership needs to understand this month, before touching the data, rather than starting from everything that happens to be available.

2
Combine evidence

Bring media, social, web, audience and institutional signals into one analytical view, so they can be weighed against each other instead of reported side by side.

3
Look for relationships

Identify where signals reinforce, contradict or qualify one another, described in terms of alignment and coincidence, not causation.

high attention but weak audience action strong leadership visibility among a small but strategically important audience sustained coverage without corresponding engagement a narrative gaining traction across several channels at once
4
Apply judgment

Separate noteworthy change from routine movement, and identify the few findings that genuinely deserve leadership attention.

5
Build the narrative

Turn the findings into a concise executive readout: what changed, why it may matter, what the evidence does and does not show, and what teams should examine or do next.

Executive readout

Two developments matter this month: a policy narrative is broadening beyond its usual audience, and a fundraising moment reached far more people than it engaged. Neither is a crisis; both change what to prioritise next.

What changed

Coverage of a policy position moved from specialist outlets into general and regional media, and the same framing began appearing in social conversation. Separately, a campaign moment drew unusually high reach with below-benchmark engagement.

Why it matters

The policy narrative is aligned with a shift in who is discussing the organisation, a broader audience forming an impression. The reach-without-engagement pattern suggests the message travelled but did not yet prompt action; the evidence does not show why, only that the two moved together.

What to watch
› Whether the policy framing stabilises or keeps broadening
› Whether engagement recovers as the campaign matures
› An early, low-volume narrative not yet strong enough to be a finding
Recommended next step

Examine the campaign audience journey before drawing conclusions about the message; prepare a clear position on the policy framing while the broader audience is still forming its view.

1

Readout first. The two or three developments that matter lead the brief; everything below is support.

2

Change, then meaning. What moved is separated from why it may matter, so leaders can weigh the interpretation.

3

Honest about limits. Language stays at "aligned with" and "suggests" — what the evidence does and does not show is explicit.

4

Watch ≠ conclusion. Emerging signals are held apart until they are strong enough to become findings.

5

Ends on a decision. Each brief closes with a focused action or question, not a summary of metrics.

The value was not more data. It was a shared and defensible interpretation of what the data meant.

Principles for responsible interpretation

"A change is not a finding until it survives context. A finding is not a conclusion until the evidence can carry it."

— reach, engagement, reputation and operational relevance are kept distinct; correlation is described as coincidence or alignment, never attribution. AI and automation speed the assembly of evidence; the judgment stays human.
What the approach made possible
  • A repeatable way to connect previously fragmented evidence
  • Reporting shifted from isolated channel metrics toward decision-relevant interpretation
  • A concise view of communications and reputation developments for senior leadership
  • A clearer line between meaningful signals and routine noise
  • A shared analytical structure for discussing performance, reputation and emerging risk
  • Input into a broader communications measurement framework for the 2027–2028 planning cycle
What the work demonstrates

Translating a broad leadership problem into research questions; synthesising qualitative and quantitative evidence; designing a recurring research and measurement product; identifying the audiences and narratives that matter; communicating uncertainty; turning findings into tactical and strategic recommendations; presenting complex analysis clearly to senior stakeholders; managing inputs across teams; and using AI where it helps without outsourcing the judgment.
